# String Quartet: Criteria of Excellence
## A Comprehensive Guide Based on World-Class Ensembles Across Eras

**Reference Ensembles:** Alban Berg, Artemis, Tokyo, Juilliard, Ébène, Takács, Amadeus, Borodin, Emerson, Kronos, Danish, Hagen

---

## 1. CROSS-ERA COMPARATIVE ANALYSIS

### **CLASSICAL ERA (c. 1750–1820)**
**Exemplars:** Haydn, Mozart, Early Beethoven

**Defining Characteristics:**
- **Structure:** Clear formal architecture (sonata-allegro, rondo, theme & variations)
- **Voice-Leading:** Contrapuntal clarity with balanced four-part writing
- **Balance:** Equal distribution of melodic interest across all four instruments
- **Harmony:** Functional tonality with clear cadences
- **Texture:** Primarily homophonic with strategic polyphonic passages
- **Register:** Careful spacing—avoiding overcrowding, maintaining clarity
- **Articulation:** Clear, precise markings defining character
- **Motivic Development:** Systematic transformation of themes
- **Dynamics:** Moderate range (p–f), used structurally
- **Techniques:** Standard bowing, occasional pizzicato

**Key Lessons:**
- Every voice has purpose—no "filler" parts
- Motivic economy—develop few ideas thoroughly
- Structural clarity enables expressive freedom

---

### **ROMANTIC ERA (c. 1820–1900)**
**Exemplars:** Late Beethoven, Schubert, Brahms, Tchaikovsky, Dvořák

**Defining Characteristics:**
- **Structure:** Expanded forms, cyclic structures, programmatic elements
- **Voice-Leading:** Rich chromaticism, complex counterpoint
- **Balance:** Dramatic shifts—solo passages, tutti climaxes
- **Harmony:** Extended tonality, modal mixture, chromatic harmony
- **Texture:** Dense polyphonic passages alternating with lyrical homophony
- **Register:** Extreme ranges exploited for emotional impact
- **Articulation:** Highly detailed—legato, marcato, portato, staccato
- **Motivic Development:** Cyclic themes, thematic transformation
- **Dynamics:** Full range (ppp–fff), extensive crescendos/diminuendos
- **Techniques:** Expanded bowing, occasional harmonics

**Key Lessons:**
- Emotional narrative drives structure
- Dynamic shaping creates dramatic arc
- Register extremes enhance expression
- Chromatic voice-leading adds depth

---

### **IMPRESSIONIST ERA (c. 1890–1920)**
**Exemplars:** Debussy, Ravel

**Defining Characteristics:**
- **Structure:** Free forms, cyclic elements, atmospheric sections
- **Voice-Leading:** Parallel motion, planing, modal scales
- **Balance:** Textural color over melodic prominence
- **Harmony:** Modal scales, whole-tone, quartal/quintal harmony
- **Texture:** Layered, atmospheric—often homophonic with coloristic effects
- **Register:** Wide spacing, exploitation of string colors
- **Articulation:** Subtle, nuanced—sul tasto, flautando
- **Motivic Development:** Fragmented, transformed through timbre
- **Dynamics:** Subtle gradations, extensive use of p–mp range
- **Techniques:** Sul tasto, sul ponticello, harmonics, flautando

**Key Lessons:**
- Timbre as primary compositional element
- Atmosphere over narrative
- Subtle dynamic control essential
- Extended techniques integrated organically

---

### **20TH-CENTURY ERA (c. 1900–2000)**
**Exemplars:** Bartók, Shostakovich, Schoenberg, Berg, Webern, Carter

**Defining Characteristics:**
- **Structure:** Symmetrical forms, palindromes, complex architectures
- **Voice-Leading:** Atonal, serial, or folk-influenced linear writing
- **Balance:** Extreme contrasts—solo vs. tutti, sparse vs. dense
- **Harmony:** Atonality, serialism, quartal/quintal, folk modes
- **Texture:** Maximum variety—pointillism to dense clusters
- **Register:** Extreme exploitation, wide leaps, registral shifts
- **Articulation:** Highly specific—marcato, martelé, spiccato, col legno
- **Motivic Development:** Serial transformation, folk-derived cells
- **Dynamics:** Extreme range, sudden contrasts, complex gradations
- **Techniques:** Extended techniques standard—col legno, sul ponticello, harmonics, pizzicato, glissandi

**Key Lessons:**
- Technique serves structure
- Rhythmic complexity drives energy
- Extended techniques expand palette
- Form and content integrated

---

### **CONTEMPORARY ERA (c. 2000–present)**
**Exemplars:** Dutilleux, Reich, Glass, Shaw, Ichmouratov, Adès

**Defining Characteristics:**
- **Structure:** Hybrid forms, multimedia, cross-genre influences
- **Voice-Leading:** Eclectic—tonal, modal, atonal, microtonal
- **Balance:** Flexible roles—instruments as equals or specialists
- **Harmony:** Spectral, modal, minimalist, or neo-tonal
- **Texture:** Maximum variety—minimalist to complex polyphony
- **Register:** Full exploitation, often extreme
- **Articulation:** Highly detailed, often experimental
- **Motivic Development:** Minimalist cells, spectral transformations, or narrative-driven
- **Dynamics:** Full range, often with electronic amplification
- **Techniques:** All extended techniques, often combined

**Key Lessons:**
- Eclecticism with purpose
- Technique and expression unified
- Cross-cultural influences enrich palette
- Technology expands possibilities

## 2. UNIFIED CRITERIA OF EXCELLENCE

### **A. VOICE-LEADING STRATEGIES**

**Excellence Indicators:**
- **Contrapuntal Clarity:** Each voice maintains independence while contributing to harmony
- **Smooth Motion:** Stepwise motion preferred; leaps justified by register or expression
- **Avoid Parallels:** Careful handling of parallel fifths/octaves (unless intentional)
- **Bass Foundation:** Cello provides harmonic foundation; viola bridges registers
- **Melodic Distribution:** Melodic interest rotates among instruments
- **Voice Crossing:** Intentional and clear, not accidental
- **Range Management:** Each instrument stays in comfortable, expressive range

**Common Pitfalls:**
- Static bass lines (cello only playing roots)
- Overlapping registers creating muddiness
- Violin I always carrying melody
- Voice-leading that ignores string fingerings

---

### **B. BALANCE, BLEND, DYNAMIC SHAPING**

**Excellence Indicators:**
- **Equal Partnership:** All four instruments contribute meaningfully
- **Dynamic Hierarchy:** Clear primary/secondary voice relationships
- **Blend:** Homogeneous sound when tutti; clarity when solo
- **Dynamic Architecture:** Crescendos/diminuendos shape phrases and sections
- **Solo Passages:** Marked clearly with supporting dynamic reduction
- **Tutti Balance:** Full ensemble maintains clarity of individual lines
- **Gradations:** Subtle dynamic changes (ppp–fff) used expressively

**Common Pitfalls:**
- Violin I always loudest
- Missing dynamic markings
- No crescendo/diminuendo shaping
- Tutti passages that obscure individual voices

---

### **C. REGISTER DISTRIBUTION**

**Excellence Indicators:**
- **Tessitura Awareness:** Each instrument in optimal range for character
- **Spacing:** Clear vertical spacing (avoid overcrowding)
- **Register Rotation:** Melodic material moves through different registers
- **Extreme Registers:** Used purposefully, not gratuitously
- **Bass Support:** Cello provides foundation; viola bridges middle
- **Upper Register:** Violins exploit high positions for intensity
- **Register Contrast:** Strategic shifts create textural variety

**Instrument Ranges (Comfortable/Extended):**
- **Violin I:** G3–E6 / G3–A7
- **Violin II:** G3–D6 / G3–G7
- **Viola:** C3–A5 / C3–C7
- **Cello:** C2–C5 / C2–A5

**Common Pitfalls:**
- Cello always in low register
- Violins always in high register
- Overlapping registers creating muddiness
- Ignoring instrument strengths

---

### **D. IDIOMATIC WRITING FOR EACH INSTRUMENT**

**Violin I:**
- **Strengths:** Melodic lines, high register intensity, rapid passagework, double stops
- **Idiomatic:** Stepwise motion, string crossings, position shifts
- **Avoid:** Constant extreme high register, awkward string crossings

**Violin II:**
- **Strengths:** Melodic support, harmonic filling, countermelodies, middle register
- **Idiomatic:** Similar to Violin I but often in supporting role
- **Avoid:** Always doubling Violin I, never having independent material

**Viola:**
- **Strengths:** Middle voice harmony, inner voice melodies, warm timbre, C-string depth
- **Idiomatic:** Exploit C-string (C3–G3) for depth, middle register for warmth
- **Avoid:** Always in alto range, never featured, awkward high positions

**Cello:**
- **Strengths:** Bass foundation, melodic lines (especially in tenor register C3–G4), pizzicato, double stops
- **Idiomatic:** Open strings for resonance, position shifts, thumb position for high register
- **Avoid:** Always in low register, only playing roots, never melodic

**Common Pitfalls:**
- Writing for "four violins" instead of four distinct instruments
- Ignoring instrument-specific strengths
- Awkward fingerings/positions
- Not exploiting unique timbres

---

### **E. PHRASING AND ARTICULATION FINGERPRINTS**

**Excellence Indicators:**
- **Slur Groupings:** Match bowing to phrase structure
- **Articulation Variety:** Legato, staccato, marcato, portato, tenuto
- **Bowing Instructions:** Up-bow/down-bow when critical
- **Phrase Markings:** Clear phrase boundaries
- **Articulation Consistency:** Motifs have consistent articulation "fingerprints"
- **Expressive Markings:** Legato, espressivo, dolce, pesante, etc.

**Articulation Types:**
- **Legato:** Smooth, connected (slurs)
- **Staccato:** Short, detached
- **Marcato:** Accented, marked
- **Portato:** Carried, slightly separated
- **Tenuto:** Sustained, held
- **Martelé:** Hammered, accented
- **Spiccato:** Bounced bow
- **Col legno:** With wood of bow
- **Pizzicato:** Plucked

**Common Pitfalls:**
- Missing articulation markings
- Inconsistent articulation for motifs
- Slurs that ignore phrase structure
- No bowing guidance

---

### **F. TEXTURE TYPES AND DENSITY MANAGEMENT**

**Texture Types:**
1. **Monophonic:** Single line (rare, dramatic)
2. **Homophonic:** Melody + accompaniment
3. **Polyphonic:** Multiple independent lines
4. **Heterophonic:** Variations of same line
5. **Pointillistic:** Isolated notes/events
6. **Cluster:** Dense, close spacing

**Density Management:**
- **Sparse:** 1–2 voices active
- **Medium:** 3 voices active
- **Dense:** All 4 voices active
- **Variety:** Strategic shifts create interest

**Excellence Indicators:**
- **Textural Contrast:** Alternation between textures
- **Density Gradation:** Build from sparse to dense
- **Clarity:** Dense textures maintain voice clarity
- **Purpose:** Texture serves expression

**Common Pitfalls:**
- Constant tutti (no textural variety)
- Dense textures that obscure lines
- No textural planning
- Texture changes without purpose

---

### **G. MOTIVIC DEVELOPMENT AND THEMATIC CLARITY**

**Excellence Indicators:**
- **Clear Presentation:** Themes introduced clearly
- **Systematic Development:** Motifs transformed logically
- **Recognition:** Themes remain recognizable through transformation
- **Economy:** Few ideas developed thoroughly
- **Cyclic Elements:** Themes return across movements
- **Variation:** Same material presented differently

**Development Techniques:**
- **Sequencing:** Motif transposed
- **Inversion:** Upside-down
- **Retrograde:** Backwards
- **Augmentation:** Longer note values
- **Diminution:** Shorter note values
- **Fragmentation:** Breaking into smaller units
- **Combination:** Multiple motifs combined

**Common Pitfalls:**
- Too many themes (lack of focus)
- Themes not developed
- Unclear thematic presentation
- Random material without connection

---

### **H. USE OF DISSONANCE/TENSION/RELEASE**

**Excellence Indicators:**
- **Purposeful Dissonance:** Creates tension, drives forward
- **Resolution:** Dissonance resolves (or intentionally doesn't)
- **Gradation:** Build tension gradually
- **Context:** Dissonance serves expression
- **Balance:** Tension and release create arc

**Tension Techniques:**
- **Harmonic:** Dissonant intervals/chords
- **Rhythmic:** Syncopation, off-beats
- **Textural:** Density increases
- **Dynamic:** Crescendo
- **Registral:** Extreme ranges
- **Timbral:** Extended techniques

**Common Pitfalls:**
- Constant dissonance (no relief)
- Dissonance without purpose
- No tension/release arc
- Abrupt changes without preparation

---

### **I. RHYTHMIC INTERPLAY AND GROOVE**

**Excellence Indicators:**
- **Syncopation:** Off-beat accents create energy
- **Polyrhythm:** Multiple rhythmic layers
- **Cross-Rhythms:** Conflicting patterns
- **Groove:** Compelling pulse, even in complex music
- **Rhythmic Motifs:** Recurring patterns
- **Coordination:** Complex rhythms remain coordinated

**Rhythmic Techniques:**
- **Syncopation:** Accent on weak beats
- **Hemiola:** 3 against 2
- **Polyrhythm:** Multiple simultaneous patterns
- **Ostinato:** Repeating pattern
- **Rhythmic Displacement:** Pattern shifted in time

**Common Pitfalls:**
- Static rhythm (no variety)
- Complex rhythms without groove
- Uncoordinated ensemble parts
- Rhythmic patterns without purpose

---

### **J. CONTEMPORARY TECHNIQUES**

**Extended Techniques:**
- **Col legno:** Wood of bow (percussive, dry)
- **Sul ponticello:** Near bridge (metallic, edgy)
- **Sul tasto:** Over fingerboard (soft, warm)
- **Harmonics:** Natural/artificial (ethereal)
- **Pizzicato:** Plucked (percussive)
- **Glissando:** Slide (smooth transition)
- **Tremolo:** Rapid bow (agitation)
- **Flautando:** Flute-like (light, airy)
- **Bartók Pizzicato:** Snapped (percussive)

**Excellence Indicators:**
- **Purposeful Use:** Techniques serve expression
- **Integration:** Techniques feel natural, not gimmicky
- **Notation:** Clear, standard notation
- **Balance:** Extended techniques balanced with standard

**Common Pitfalls:**
- Techniques used gratuitously
- Poor notation (unclear instructions)
- Overuse of extended techniques
- Techniques that don't serve music

---

### **K. EMOTIONAL AND NARRATIVE PACING**

**Excellence Indicators:**
- **Arc:** Clear emotional journey
- **Peaks/Valleys:** Dynamic and textural contrast
- **Preparation:** Climaxes prepared
- **Resolution:** Satisfying conclusion
- **Pacing:** Not too fast/slow
- **Contrast:** Variety maintains interest

**Pacing Elements:**
- **Tempo:** Speed changes
- **Dynamics:** Volume changes
- **Texture:** Density changes
- **Harmony:** Tension/release
- **Register:** High/low shifts
- **Technique:** Standard/extended

**Common Pitfalls:**
- Static pacing (no change)
- Abrupt changes without preparation
- No clear arc
- Climax too early/late

## 3. PRACTICAL COMPOSER IMPROVEMENT GUIDE

### **STEP 1: VOICE-LEADING AUDIT**

**Action Items:**
1. **Analyze Each Voice:** Does each part have purpose? Is it melodic, harmonic, or rhythmic?
2. **Check Motion:** Are voices moving smoothly? Are leaps justified?
3. **Bass Function:** Is cello providing harmonic foundation? Is it melodic when appropriate?
4. **Register Spacing:** Are voices clearly separated? Avoid overlapping in same octave
5. **Voice Rotation:** Does melodic interest move among instruments?

**Specific Revisions:**
- [ ] Eliminate "filler" parts—every note has purpose
- [ ] Ensure cello has melodic passages (not just bass)
- [ ] Check voice-leading for parallel fifths/octaves
- [ ] Verify each instrument has independent material
- [ ] Space voices clearly (avoid muddiness)

---

### **STEP 2: BALANCE AND DYNAMIC ARCHITECTURE**

**Action Items:**
1. **Dynamic Markings:** Add comprehensive dynamics (ppp–fff)
2. **Crescendos/Diminuendos:** Shape phrases and sections
3. **Solo Passages:** Mark clearly with supporting dynamic reduction
4. **Tutti Balance:** Ensure clarity in full ensemble
5. **Dynamic Arc:** Plan overall dynamic shape of movement

**Specific Revisions:**
- [ ] Add dynamic marking every 4–8 measures minimum
- [ ] Add crescendo/diminuendo hairpins for phrase shaping
- [ ] Mark solo passages with reduced dynamics in other parts
- [ ] Ensure tutti passages maintain voice clarity
- [ ] Create dynamic arc: opening → development → climax → resolution

---

### **STEP 3: REGISTER DISTRIBUTION REVIEW**

**Action Items:**
1. **Tessitura Check:** Is each instrument in optimal range?
2. **Register Rotation:** Does melodic material move through registers?
3. **Spacing:** Are voices clearly spaced vertically?
4. **Extreme Registers:** Used purposefully?
5. **Instrument Strengths:** Exploiting unique timbres?

**Specific Revisions:**
- [ ] Move cello to tenor register (C3–G4) for melodic passages
- [ ] Exploit viola C-string (C3–G3) for depth
- [ ] Use violin high register (E5+) for intensity, not constantly
- [ ] Ensure clear vertical spacing (avoid overcrowding)
- [ ] Rotate melodic material through different registers

---

### **STEP 4: IDIOMATIC WRITING CHECK**

**Action Items:**
1. **Violin I:** Melodic lines, high register, rapid passagework
2. **Violin II:** Supporting melodies, harmonic filling, countermelodies
3. **Viola:** Middle voice harmony, inner melodies, C-string depth
4. **Cello:** Bass foundation, melodic lines (tenor register), pizzicato

**Specific Revisions:**
- [ ] Ensure Violin II has independent material (not just doubling)
- [ ] Give viola featured passages (exploit warm timbre)
- [ ] Write cello melodies in tenor register (C3–G4)
- [ ] Check fingerings—avoid awkward positions
- [ ] Exploit instrument-specific strengths (pizzicato cello, viola C-string)

---

### **STEP 5: ARTICULATION AND PHRASING**

**Action Items:**
1. **Slur Groupings:** Match bowing to phrase structure
2. **Articulation Variety:** Add legato, staccato, marcato, portato, tenuto
3. **Motif Fingerprints:** Consistent articulation for recurring motifs
4. **Bowing Instructions:** Add up-bow/down-bow when critical
5. **Expressive Markings:** Add legato, espressivo, dolce, pesante

**Specific Revisions:**
- [ ] Add articulation markings to all motifs
- [ ] Ensure slurs match phrase structure
- [ ] Create articulation "fingerprints" for themes
- [ ] Add expressive markings (espressivo, dolce, etc.)
- [ ] Mark bowing when critical for coordination

---

### **STEP 6: TEXTURE AND DENSITY PLANNING**

**Action Items:**
1. **Texture Map:** Identify texture types throughout movement
2. **Density Gradation:** Plan sparse → medium → dense
3. **Textural Contrast:** Alternate homophonic/polyphonic
4. **Clarity:** Ensure dense textures maintain voice clarity
5. **Purpose:** Verify texture serves expression

**Specific Revisions:**
- [ ] Create textural map: identify sections by texture type
- [ ] Add sparse sections (1–2 voices) for contrast
- [ ] Ensure dense sections (4 voices) maintain clarity
- [ ] Plan textural gradation: build from sparse to dense
- [ ] Alternate textures for variety

---

### **STEP 7: MOTIVIC DEVELOPMENT AUDIT**

**Action Items:**
1. **Theme Identification:** List all themes/motifs
2. **Development Check:** Are themes developed systematically?
3. **Recognition:** Do themes remain recognizable?
4. **Economy:** Too many themes? Focus on few, develop thoroughly
5. **Cyclic Elements:** Themes return/transform?

**Specific Revisions:**
- [ ] Identify primary themes (should be 2–3 maximum)
- [ ] Develop themes through sequencing, inversion, fragmentation
- [ ] Ensure themes remain recognizable through transformation
- [ ] Eliminate or integrate extraneous material
- [ ] Create cyclic returns of themes

---

### **STEP 8: TENSION/RELEASE ARCHITECTURE**

**Action Items:**
1. **Tension Map:** Identify points of tension/release
2. **Gradation:** Build tension gradually
3. **Resolution:** Dissonances resolve (or intentionally don't)
4. **Balance:** Tension and release create arc
5. **Purpose:** Dissonance serves expression

**Specific Revisions:**
- [ ] Map tension/release points throughout movement
- [ ] Build tension gradually (don't peak too early)
- [ ] Ensure dissonances resolve (or are intentionally unresolved)
- [ ] Create tension/release arc: calm → tension → climax → resolution
- [ ] Verify dissonance serves expression, not gratuitous

---

### **STEP 9: RHYTHMIC INTERPLAY ENHANCEMENT**

**Action Items:**
1. **Syncopation:** Add off-beat accents
2. **Polyrhythm:** Layer multiple rhythmic patterns
3. **Groove:** Ensure compelling pulse
4. **Rhythmic Motifs:** Create recurring patterns
5. **Coordination:** Complex rhythms remain coordinated

**Specific Revisions:**
- [ ] Add syncopation for energy
- [ ] Create polyrhythmic layers (3 against 2, etc.)
- [ ] Ensure groove even in complex passages
- [ ] Develop rhythmic motifs systematically
- [ ] Verify ensemble coordination in complex rhythms

---

### **STEP 10: CONTEMPORARY TECHNIQUES INTEGRATION**

**Action Items:**
1. **Purpose:** Techniques serve expression
2. **Integration:** Techniques feel natural
3. **Notation:** Clear, standard notation
4. **Balance:** Extended techniques balanced with standard
5. **Variety:** Different techniques for different effects

**Specific Revisions:**
- [ ] Add col legno for percussive, earthy effects
- [ ] Add sul ponticello for metallic, edgy timbres
- [ ] Add sul tasto for soft, warm passages
- [ ] Add harmonics for ethereal moments
- [ ] Ensure techniques serve expression, not gimmicky

---

### **STEP 11: EMOTIONAL PACING ARCHITECTURE**

**Action Items:**
1. **Arc:** Plan emotional journey
2. **Peaks/Valleys:** Dynamic and textural contrast
3. **Preparation:** Prepare climaxes
4. **Resolution:** Satisfying conclusion
5. **Pacing:** Not too fast/slow

**Specific Revisions:**
- [ ] Map emotional arc: opening → development → climax → resolution
- [ ] Create peaks (climaxes) and valleys (contrast)
- [ ] Prepare climaxes with gradual build
- [ ] Ensure satisfying resolution
- [ ] Verify pacing—not static, not too abrupt

---

### **STEP 12: FINAL SYNTHESIS CHECKLIST**

**Comprehensive Review:**
- [ ] **Voice-Leading:** Each voice has purpose, smooth motion
- [ ] **Balance:** Equal partnership, dynamic hierarchy clear
- [ ] **Register:** Optimal tessitura, clear spacing, rotation
- [ ] **Idiomatic:** Each instrument exploited for strengths
- [ ] **Articulation:** Comprehensive markings, motif fingerprints
- [ ] **Texture:** Variety, density gradation, clarity
- [ ] **Motivic:** Clear themes, systematic development
- [ ] **Tension/Release:** Purposeful dissonance, satisfying arc
- [ ] **Rhythm:** Syncopation, groove, coordination
- [ ] **Techniques:** Extended techniques integrated purposefully
- [ ] **Pacing:** Clear emotional arc, peaks/valleys, resolution

## 4. COMPARATIVE ANALYSIS FRAMEWORK

### **When Analyzing Your Quartets:**

**For Each Movement, Ask:**

1. **Voice-Leading:** Are all four voices independent and purposeful?
2. **Balance:** Do all instruments contribute meaningfully?
3. **Register:** Is register distribution optimal?
4. **Idiomatic:** Is writing idiomatic for each instrument?
5. **Articulation:** Are articulations comprehensive and consistent?
6. **Texture:** Is there textural variety and density management?
7. **Motivic:** Are themes clear and systematically developed?
8. **Tension/Release:** Is there a clear emotional arc?
9. **Rhythm:** Is there rhythmic interplay and groove?
10. **Techniques:** Are extended techniques used purposefully?
11. **Pacing:** Is emotional pacing effective?

**Scoring System:**
- **Excellent (5):** Meets world-class standards
- **Good (4):** Strong but could be refined
- **Adequate (3):** Functional but needs improvement
- **Needs Work (2):** Significant issues
- **Critical (1):** Major problems

**Target:** Aim for 4–5 in all categories

## 5. QUICK REFERENCE: COMMON ISSUES AND SOLUTIONS

| Issue | Solution |
|------|----------|
| Violin I always melody | Rotate melodic material among instruments |
| Cello only bass | Write cello melodies in tenor register (C3–G4) |
| Missing dynamics | Add dynamic marking every 4–8 measures |
| No textural variety | Alternate sparse (1–2 voices) and dense (4 voices) |
| Too many themes | Focus on 2–3 themes, develop thoroughly |
| Static rhythm | Add syncopation, polyrhythm, rhythmic motifs |
| No tension/release | Map dissonance/resolution, build arc |
| Awkward fingerings | Check instrument ranges, avoid extreme positions |
| Dense textures muddy | Space voices clearly, reduce density |
| No extended techniques | Add col legno, sul ponticello, harmonics purposefully |
